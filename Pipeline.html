WHAT IS THE PIPELINE?

A pipeline is basically a way of facilitating work amougst a group of people in order to create a product. In the context of the Animation Industry, this pipeline consists of several roles. Each piece of work is used by everyone in production and has to be essentially built off of in order to progress. If even one of these roles faulter, then the whole production gets held up.

PRE-PRODUCTION

This is the stage at which the Animation Committee greenlights a potential project. Most anime being made are adaptions of comics or manga through Shueisha/Shonen Jump or other publishers. Once the committee makes their decision, a studio is offered the project alongside a producer and director. Usually all of this happens roughly 2-3 years before the first episode of the work releases. Preproduction is where the character designs, script, and concept work is made. Many animators use these in order to adapt the directors vision accurately, so a good supply of materials is important for a healthy production.

STORYBOARDING/コンテ & PLANNING

After the staff is chosen, the director(and sometimes the assistant director as well) will begin storyboarding the scenes, making revisions and discussing ideas with the assistant director, producers (production desk), and main staff (animators). Many studios have their own way of handling storyboards, for example the Upcoming Mononoke movie uses a lot of 3D assets to sell the perspective and compositions, while productions like Jujutsu Kaisen S3 and One Piece use rougher boards. Sometimes these approaches are due to either time constraints or wanting the animators to take creative liberties.

LAYOUT/ROUGH GENGA(ラフ原画)

This is where the rough animation phase takes place. Production Desk/Production Assistants each have their cluster or group of animators who are in charge of their own sections. Layout is where the animator lays out the base foundation of the cut, being very rough but structural in the way that they draw, These artists have creative liberties on how they want to portray the motion and typically focus on the movement of the subjects.

ANIMATION DIRECTOR REVISION/アニメーション監督/総作画監督

There are several directorial roles when it comes to anime: Story Director (Either the Series Director or Episode Director), Animation Director, and Chief Animation Director (Usually the person who drew the character designs). These artists are in charge of doing draw overs on top of the LO, sometimes making the drawings more on model and even fixing continuity errors, this is the stage where the drawings really matter, so the most skilled artist usually handles this role

GENGA (KEY ANIMATION)/原画

After the director check stage, someone has to go in and apply these new changes. There are 2 types of operations when it comes to cleaning up rough animation work, Genga and Nigen. If the original artist who did the LO comes back to clean the work, it is called Genga, but if another artist comes in to clean the work, then it is referred as Nigen. However the final result will always be called Genga. It is usually preferred if the original artist comes back to do the Genga, because not only do they understand the nuance of the lines and can add new creative changes if experienced enough, but its good work ethic to essentially clean up the rough work that was made. Sometimes if the Genga work is lacking, then the Chief animation director comes back to fix the work referred to as Genga Correction (Genga Sakkan).

DOUGA (INBETWEENING)/動画

When the completed genga is complete, we can't start coloring the drawings due to the messy linework so another artist comes in and in-betweens the frames and traces over the Genga. These artist are referred to as the Dougaman, which is considered an entry level position alongside Nigen artist work.' The job of the dougaman can be strenuous since they have to trace over hundreds and inbetween thousands of cels a month in order to prepare for coloring. Similar to the Chief Animation Director, A Douga Kensa/Douga Director checks each cel to make sure there aren't any inconsistencies or stray marks, this step is important since this is the final line that the viewer will see on TV!

COLORING/仕上げ

The coloring or Shiage stage is simple, color artists take the pixel drawings made by the Douga team and color them based on a shiage sheet or coloring chart provided. This department also has their own version of Douga Kensa which checks each cel to make sure its ready for the final photography

COMPOSITING (PHOTOGRAPHY)/最終撮影

Once all of the cels have been checked and colored, then the compositing/photography team edits and compile all of the drawings, backgrounds and extra elements together. Sometimes the Director and even the LO animator provide compositing references for them to follow.

REVIEW AND CLOSURE

After editing and thorough checks from the editing team and the director, the final episode is sent out for publishing and the process continues all over again On average about 12-13 episodes are produced per season and many studios work on multiple episodes at once, so the team isn't only focused on one part of the pipeline. There are more people nowadays working within this industry, bringing their own creative way of expressing themselves compared to back then. A lot of freelance jobs in the anime industry were taken by people who lived natively in Japan, but with the growth of social media and demand increase, freelance animators from all over the world take some of the workload of these major studios
